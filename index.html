Magnesium Fluoride



How is Magnesium Fluoride Formed?

We can see from this diagram how magnesium fluoride is formed, with an ionic bond. Ionic bonds occur between metals and non metals, and involve the “trading”, or taking and giving of electrons. The metal in the compound, here being aluminum, gives up electrons, and the non metal (fluorine) takes them. They want to do this to make sure their outermost electron shell, the valence shell, is full of electrons. Metals give up electrons until the original valence shell has no electrons; it doesn’t exist, making the next shell inwards the valence shell, which will be full. Non metals want to fill up the outermost valence shell instead. But the resulting compound should be neutral. So because magnesium wants to give up two electrons according to the diagram, and fluorine only needs one electron, we need two fluorine ions to balance the compound out, to make sure that each atom has a full valence shell. When these electrons are traded, the resulting ion is positive if it's a metal, and negative if it is a non-metal, and although overall the compound is neutral, these individual ions have different charges. We know that opposite charges attract each other, and so that attraction/bond between ions keeps the compound together.

How is Magnesium Fluoride Used?

Magnesium fluoride’s main use is in optics, as it is a material that is transparent over a relatively wide range of the electromagnetic spectrum, from infrared to ultraviolet (including the visual spectrum we can see). This means that windows, lenses or prisms, as well as any application requiring transparency along these wavelengths can use magnesium fluoride. But one of its main applications is in the vacuum ultraviolet range, at 121 nanometers, because it is one of only 2 known materials that can transmit light at that level, the other being lithium fluoride. It is quite expensive at 3$ per gram, but it is highly specialized which is a contributing factor. It is also used in multilayer coatings, which are ways to combine attractive properties of different materials if properties of one aren’t enough, as well as anti-reflective coatings, which reduces most reflections of light. There are anti-reflective glasses coatings that are made to reduce glare in your glasses, in fact showing its use to general society. These lenses may cost upwards of 100$ though.



Hazards.

Skin Irritation

Magnesium fluoride can cause some skin irritation, so you should:

  • Wash hands thoroughly.
  • Use soap.
  • Wear protective gloves.

Respiratary Irritation

Magnesium fluoride can also cause respiratory irritation, so you should:

  • Avoid breathing it's dust.
  • Go outside to fresh air.
  • Wear a mask.

Eye Irritation

The most serious of the irritations, in this case you should:

  • Don't rub your eyes without washing.
  • Rinse eyes for several minutes.
  • Wear eye protection.


In every case, if you feel unwell, CALL POISON CONTROL.
